01

Abstract

This study investigates the impact of urban form—specifically building alignment—on energy consumption in residential areas. While most research emphasizes reducing energy demand at the individual building level, this work adopts a broader, city-scale perspective. It examines how the spatial configuration of buildings influences thermal loads, offering a new approach to improving urban energy efficiency.

The research centers on a prototypical urban block of 16 midrise residential buildings in Tehran, a city still experiencing rapid development. Through parametric modeling and energy simulation, three alignment scenarios (X, Y, and XY) are evaluated for their thermal performance. Findings reveal that even minor variations in building layout—such as adjusted spacing or slight misalignments—can significantly influence heating and cooling demands. Notably, combined misalignment in both X and Y directions can reduce primary energy consumption by up to 2.4%, primarily due to changes in solar access and shading. The study demonstrates how strategic urban form can yield measurable energy savings at scale.

Research Problem

Context: Urban Form

As urbanization accelerates, the role of urban form in achieving sustainable development becomes increasingly vital. By 2050, nearly two-thirds of the global population is projected to live in cities, intensifying challenges related to energy demand, climate change, and resource depletion. Yet, much of the current research still concentrates on individual buildings, often overlooking the broader spatial organization of urban environments.

Urban form—the physical arrangement of buildings, streets, and open spaces—profoundly affects environmental performance. Factors such as solar access, wind flow, shading, and microclimate are directly influenced by how buildings are positioned relative to one another. These spatial relationships significantly impact energy use. A deeper understanding of urban form can empower planners and designers to make data-informed decisions that improve energy efficiency and resilience at the scale that matters most: the city.

Research Gap: Building Scale vs. Urban Scale

Much of the literature on building energy efficiency focuses on the individual building level, emphasizing high-performance or zero-energy structures. While effective at the micro scale, these solutions often involve complex design, construction, and maintenance processes. More importantly, their cumulative effect on urban-scale energy consumption is often marginal.

For example, estimates of residential energy use in Tehran place the baseline demand at approximately 78,114,241 MWh. Even if the city adopted zero-energy residential buildings at the same rate as the U.S. and Canada, the resulting reduction in total energy use would be only 0.87%—a modest improvement given the effort required.

This reveals a critical research gap: individual energy-efficient buildings alone cannot drive significant change at the urban level. Our study therefore explores broader strategies that offer moderate savings per unit but can be implemented at scale with lower complexity. Specifically, we investigate how optimizing building alignment within urban blocks—leveraging passive design principles—can enhance energy performance across neighborhoods and cities. By shifting focus from isolated buildings to systemic spatial strategies, this research aims to identify scalable, cost-effective interventions for reducing urban energy use in rapidly growing cities.

Research Questions

1. What are the different types of building alignment in urban blocks, and how are they defined?

This question establishes a methodological framework that could be applied to other cities, creating a standardized approach to urban form analysis.

2. What correlation exists between the alignment of neighboring blocks and their thermal loads?

Answering this will demonstrate the importance of studying building alignment as a strategy for urban energy reduction. Three types of correlation may emerge:

  • Positive correlation: As alignment increases, thermal load increases proportionally
  • Negative correlation: Improved alignment leads to reduced thermal loads
  • Neutral correlation: No predictable relationship between alignment and thermal performance

3. How does building alignment correlate with specific heating and cooling demands?

This analysis will provide actionable insights for decision-making in urban planning and design.

4. To what extent does the alignment-thermal load correlation align with baseline energy patterns?

The answer to this question will inform policy decisions across different countries with varying electricity generation technologies, suggesting how universal these urban design principles may be.

These questions collectively address the critical gap in urban-scale energy efficiency strategies, moving beyond individual building optimization to examine how spatial relationships between buildings affect energy performance at neighborhood and city scales.

03

Methodology

1. Alignment Definitions

Reviewed historical definitions of alignment, from 2D point grids (1980) to urban design applications (2013). An appropriate definition was selected based on the research goals and questions.

2. Climate-Based Alignment Definition

Developed climate-responsive alignment metrics. While previous definitions were based on Gestalt theory or abstract mathematics, this study introduces a precise mathematical definition tailored for climatic and energy performance analysis.

3. Generative and Discriminative Models

Created two complementary models: (1) A generative model to produce urban blocks with controlled alignment values (ranging from 0 = disaligned to 1 = fully aligned); (2) A discriminative model using edge detection and vector analysis to quantify alignment in real-world urban fabrics.

4. Alternative Dataset Creation

Generated multiple urban block configurations with systematic variations in alignment (X, Y, and XY axes; values from 0.0 to 1.0 in 0.1 steps). Each layout was tagged with geometric data and compiled into a clean, non-repetitive dataset.

5. Evaluation Model

Established an automated simulation pipeline using UrbanOpt and Dragonfly to assess annual heating and cooling loads for each alignment scenario. Results were recorded alongside their respective parameters.

6. Data Post-Processing

Applied data cleaning procedures to filter out simulation anomalies while preserving valid alignment variations. Results were normalized across climate zones to support consistent comparison.

7. Correlation Analysis and Trend Detection

Analyzed the relationship between alignment and energy demand using: (1) Pearson correlation matrices, (2) alignment threshold analyses, and (3) multivariate regression models accounting for layout category.

8. Design Principles and Recommendations

Derived urban design principles from the findings, including alignment-based strategies applicable to Tehran. Final recommendations were evaluated in the context of the original research scenarios.

04

Key Findings

Finding 1: Misalignment Reduces Energy Consumption

Introducing controlled misalignment in both X and Y axes can lead to measurable energy savings. The study shows that full misalignment (alignment value = 0.0) can reduce primary energy use by up to 2.4%, primarily due to improved solar exposure, better shading performance, and diversified thermal behavior across the urban block.

Finding 2: Uniform Layouts Perform the Worst

Across all tested scenarios, layouts with uniform building distribution exhibited the highest thermal loads. This finding emphasizes the impact of spatial variety on environmental performance, supporting the need for differentiated alignment strategies at the city scale.

Finding 3: Urban Form Scenario Shows Greater Impact at Scale

Comparison of the two main scenarios—(1) adopting zero-energy buildings at the rate seen in the U.S. and Canada, and (2) optimizing building alignment in 70% of Tehran’s residential areas—reveals a striking difference. The first scenario leads to a negligible reduction of 0.0084% in total residential energy use, despite the high cost and complexity involved. The second scenario, focused on spatial alignment, results in an estimated 2.5% reduction in baseline energy demand.

05

Discussion

Interpretation

These findings demonstrate that building alignment is not merely a visual or planning consideration—it directly influences thermal performance. The results challenge the conventional focus on isolated building design by highlighting the collective energy benefits of urban-scale spatial decisions. However, the study reveals three critical gaps in current urban energy analysis:

  1. Lack of comprehensive methodology: No unified framework exists for analyzing energy consumption patterns at city scale, particularly for alignment-based configurations
  2. Indicator ambiguity: Metrics like EUI (Energy Use Intensity) and Primary Energy serve different purposes - where EUI reflects household consumption/costs, Primary Energy relates to macro-level policy and environmental impact - without clear translation between them
  3. Definitional inconsistencies: Key geometric parameters (alignment, density, compactness) lack architecturally operational definitions applicable across planning scales

Implications

This study suggests a new design approach: incorporating alignment variability into zoning and planning regulations. The proposed framework addresses current methodological gaps by providing:

  • Architecturally meaningful geometric parameters
  • Scalable analysis from buildings to districts
  • Clear policy translation pathways

Urban planners can leverage these alignment-based strategies to:

  • Develop form-based energy codes
  • Optimize solar access through 3D zoning
  • Balance density with energy performance

Limitations

The simulations are based on prototypical blocks and climate conditions specific to Tehran (ASHRAE Zone 3). Further research is needed to:

  • Validate findings across diverse urban contexts and climates
  • Test mixed-use and high-rise configurations
  • Develop standardized geometric definitions for urban energy studies
  • Establish protocols for translating simulation results to planning codes

Key Insight

Sustainable urban development may depend less on isolated zero-energy buildings and more on optimizing urban form at city scale. This shift requires rethinking conventional planning paradigms to prioritize geometric relationships between buildings as a primary energy-saving strategy.
